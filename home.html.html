When the Rain Fell

A Saturday night, Aria got a text from her boyfriend. Just six words, sitting quietly on her screen: Can we talk? It’s important.

Her heart didn’t race — it dropped. She stared at the message for a long moment, already knowing that nothing good ever started with those words. Outside, the city buzzed with weekend laughter, but inside her apartment, everything felt too still. She typed Sure. then deleted it. Typed What’s wrong? then deleted that too.

Finally, she left him on read — like silence might protect her from what was coming.

Needing a distraction, she headed to the bar to grab drinks for her and her mates. Hey, three tequilas, please, she said, trying to sound casual.

Yeah, no worries — coming right up, the bartender replied.

As she waited, her thoughts returned to the text. It echoed in her head like a warning bell. She stood there, numb, confused, fingers tapping lightly against the bar top. Her brain began to spiral — Did I do something? Is he okay? Is he breaking up with me?

Then she caught herself. Stop overthinking, she told herself. It might be something else. Maybe it’s nothing.

But deep down, she knew. No one says It’s important unless it is.

Here are your three tequilas, the bartender said, sliding the glasses across the bar.

Thank you, Aria replied softly.

She took the drinks in both hands and walked back to her friends like nothing had happened — like her chest wasn’t tight, like her mind wasn’t a storm of questions she couldn’t silence.

The music pulsed, laughter rose around her, and someone bumped into her shoulder with a clumsy Cheers! But Aria just smiled, nodding along, the same way she always did.

The smile wasn’t real.
It never really was.

After a long night out with her mates, Aria said her goodbyes and stepped out into the cool night air. The streets were quieter now, a breeze brushing past her as if the world already knew what was coming.

She stared at her phone for a moment before finally tapping his name and hitting call. The ringing felt louder than it should.

“Hey,” she said when he picked up. Her voice was soft, unsure. “Uhm… you said you wanted to talk?”

A pause. Then, without hesitation:

“Yeah. I… I want to break up.”

Aria stopped walking. Just like that, the night stilled.

“Oh,” she said, barely above a whisper.

“I’m sorry,” he continued, his voice flat. “This just… isn’t working out.”

“Yeah. Yeah, no — you’re right,” she replied, swallowing the lump in her throat.

“Goodbye, Aria. Take care.”

The call ended before she could say anything else. Just a cold silence where warmth used to be.

She stood there as the rain began to fall — soft at first, then heavier, like the sky was mourning with her.

Tears slipped down her face, warm against the cold drops. She didn’t wipe them away. Didn’t move. Just stood still as the world blurred around her.

Eventually, she began walking, slowly, aimlessly, her clothes soaked and heavy. Streetlights shimmered through the rain, painting the pavement gold and lonely.

When she reached her apartment, she fumbled for her keys. Her hands were shaking too much. The metal kept missing the lock. Her vision swam.

Her chest was tight, her head spinning, her heart breaking in absolute silence.

She finally got the door open, stepped inside, and let it quietly shut behind her — like the last page of a chapter she never wanted to end.

She closed the door behind her and turned, pressing her back against it like it was the only thing holding her up.

Then she slid down slowly, the weight of it all too much to carry anymore.

Knees to her chest, arms wrapped tightly around them, she let the sobs come — heavy, broken, unstoppable.

The kind of crying that doesn’t ask for comfort. The kind that just needs to exist.

And that’s what she did. She cried.

All night.

Until the morning light crawled through the blinds and found her still there, eyes swollen, voice gone, heart shattered — and no more tears left to give.

Everyone thought she was fine.
She laughed, showed up, smiled.

But no one saw the nights she didn’t sleep.
No one heard the screams she swallowed.

Because the smile she wore...
was the one that lied.
